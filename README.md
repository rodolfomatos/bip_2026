## Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) produce text with extraordinary fluency.  
However, fluency is not the same as understanding, truth, or epistemic reliability.

This presentation explores how humans evaluate credibility, why persuasive language can create an *illusion of knowing*, and why the governance of reasoning processes matters in AI-assisted environments.

The talk connects concepts from:

- epistemology,
- critical thinking,
- persuasion theory,
- information credibility,
- AI alignment,
- and human-computer interaction,

to examine a central question:

> **How do we know that what we think we know is true?**

## Epistemic Governance Protocol

One of the central ideas proposed in this work is an **Epistemic Governance Protocol**, designed to reduce overconfidence, hidden assumptions, and hallucination-like reasoning patterns in AI-assisted interactions.

Core principles include:

1. Surface uncertainty explicitly
2. Expose reasoning structures
3. Flag assumptions before they are used
4. Declare scope boundaries
5. Invite contradiction
6. Distinguish empirical from normative claims
